Lagrange's theorem and the RSA theorem

Algebra

Groups

/ Finite groups

/ Lagrange's theorem

Four theorems

Lagrange’s theorem. The order of every subgroup of a finite group \(G\) divides the order of the group \(G\).

Lagrange’s theorem doesn’t seem too impressive. It doesn’t say anything about how many subgroups of each size there are (only that there could be). But I just learned of a beautiful application: using Lagrange’s theorem, it’s easy to prove Fermat’s little theorem and Euler’s theorem. As a kicker, these two are relevant to understanding RSA encryption.

In every field \(\Z / p\Z\), if \(a\) is nonzero then \(a^{p-1} =_p 1\).

. In every ring \(\Z / n\Z\), if \(a\) is nonzero then \(a^{\phi[n]} =_n 1\).

Using these two results, we can prove what I like to call "the RSA theorem".

. Let \(d, e \in \Z\), let \(\phi: \Z \to \Z^+\) be Euler’s totient function. If \(d \cdot e =_{\phi[n]} 1\), then \(m^{d \cdot e} =_n m\).

Here, we’ve written \(x =_n y\) what is usually written \(x \equiv y \pmod n\). The latter is read "\(x\) is congruent to \(y\) modulo \(n\)", and it means that \(x\) and \(y\) are equal in the quotient ring \(\Z / n\Z\) (they represent the same equivalence class if you prefer the language of equivalence relations, or they belong to the same coset of \(n\Z\) if you prefer the language of cosets); it also means that \(x\) and \(y\) leave the same remainder when divided by \(n\) if the computations are carried out in the parent ring \(\Z\). The notation \(x =_n y\) means the same thing, but you may read it as "\(x\) is \(n\)-equal to \(y\)" or "\(x\) is equal to \(y\) over \(\Z / n\Z\)".

The notation \(\Z / n\Z\) is read "the quotient ring of the ring \(\Z\) by the ideal \(n\Z\)", or "the quotient ring of the ring \(\Z\) modulo the ideal \(n\Z\)". Here, \(n\) is taken to be a fixed, but arbitrary integer. The set \(\Z\) is simply the set

\[\Z := \{\ldots, -3, -2, -1, 0, 1, 2, 3, \ldots\}\]

of all integers.

For a fixed integer \(n\), the set \(n\Z\) is the set

\[n\Z := \{\ldots, -3 \cdot n, -2 \cdot n, -1 \cdot n, 0 \cdot n, 1 \cdot n, 2 \cdot n, 3 \cdot n, \ldots\}\]

of all integer multiples of \(n\).

For a fixed integer \(n\), the quotient ring \(\Z / n\Z\) arises by gluing the ring \(\Z\) "through" the ideal \(n\Z\). The operationg of gluing is realized through the idea of equivalence relations and equivalence classes, and it amounts to relaxing the notion of equality by considering to be equal in the quotient ring \(\Z / n\Z\) elements that were not equal in the parent ring \(\Z\).

The fact that you can fix two arbitrary elements \(x\) and \(y\) of the ring \(\Z\) and consider them over multiple quotient rings of \(\Z\) is, in my opinion, quite deep. For example, take \(5\) and \(13\). They represent different elements over \(\Z\), but they represent the same element over the quotient ring \(\Z / 4\Z\). In the language of arithmetic in \(\Z\), \(5\) and \(13\) are equal over \(\Z / 4\Z\) because their difference is divisible by \(4\) (their difference is \(-8\)), and also because over \(\Z\) they leave the same remainder when divided by \(4\) (the remainder is \(1\)). In the language of rings, \(5\) and \(13\) are equal over \(\Z / 4\Z\) because they belong to the same coset of the ideal \(4\Z\); namely, they belong to the coset \(4\Z + 1\).

For each positive integer \(n\) you can consider the reduction of \(\Z\) modulo \(n\), which amounts to taking the quotient ring of \(\Z\) by the ideal \(n\Z\). The ring \(\Z\) is related to its quotient rings \(\Z / n\Z\) by something called canonical epimorphisms. These canonical epimorphisms allow us to take an element of \(\Z\), say \(5\), and consider \(5\) over multiple quotient rings of \(\Z\). In order to go from \(5\) as an element of \(\Z\) to \(5\) as an element of, say, \(\Z / 4\Z\), we apply the canonical epimorphism

\[\phi_4 : \Z \to \Z / 4\Z\]

to \(5\), which maps \(5 \in \Z\) to \(5 \in \Z / 4\Z\).

Reduction modulo \(n\) is particularly important when \(n\) is a prime, in which case we call it \(p\). Reduction modulo a prime ideal yields a field, and every field arises as the quotient of some ring by some prime ideal. The localization of a commutative ring at a prime ideal \(P\) yields something called a local ring \(R^*\) (which is a ring with a unique maximal ideal), and the maximal ideal of the local ring \(R^*\) is the ideal generated by \(P\) inside of \(R^*\).

At every ring \(R\), the prime ideals of \(R\) form a weird space called the prime spectrum of \(R\), denoted \({\rm Spec}[R]\). In the ring \(\Z\), each prime ideal is generated by a single prime \(p\) (but, in general, prime ideals need more than one generator), and so the prime spectrum of \(\Z\) arises from the prime numbers. I call \({\rm Spec}[R]\) "weird" because its topology deviates substantially from the topology of more familiar spaces like \(3\)-dimensional Euclidean space \({\mathbb R}^3\): \({\rm Spec}[R]\) is almost never Hausdorff! Non-Hausdorf spaces are rarely studied in analysis, even in topology itself, but they seem quite common in algebraic geometry. Moreover, the topology that comes with \({\rm Spec}[R]\), called the Zariski topology, is riddled with technical deficiencies (I often read that it "doesn’t have enough open sets", but I don’t know what this means). What you can do to get around the weakness of the Zariski topology is replace it with a much more powerful notion of topology: a Grothendieck topology called the étale topology. Under the étale topology, the set of all prime ideals of \(R\) becomes the étale spectrum of \(R\).

Understanding a bit about rings, ideals, and quotient rings is important because it’s a segway into deeper mathematics, and also because it provides a more abstract, general, and ultimately simpler language to talk about results in number theory, like Fermat’s little theorem and Euler’s theorem.

Euler’s theorem is a generalization of Fermat’s little theorem: every field \(\Z / p\Z\) is also a ring \(\Z / n\Z\), because \(n\) stands for an arbitrary integer, and every prime \(p\) is an integer. Both results are important in cryptography, specifically for RSA encryption.

What is a coset?

In group theory, cosets are some of the most important objects. A coset is a special subset of a group that arises from a subgroup. To every subgroup \(H\) of a group we can associate the set of all cosets of \(H\). You can think of the cosets of the subgroup \(H\) as "logically attached" to \(H\). That is: every time you think of a particular group \(G\), you can immediately think of the subgroups of \(G\); and every time you think of a particular subgroup \(H\), you can immediately think of the cosets of \(H\).

If you see the word coset in the wild, you need 3 pieces of data to specify the coset: \(1)\) a group \(G\), a subgroup \(H\), and an element \(g\) of \(G\). Then, the coset that these data specify is written as \(gH\). Every element \(g\) of \(G\) yields one coset \(gH\), but not all of these cosets will be different! If each element \(g\) of \(G\) were to generate a different coset \(gH\), then every subgroup \(H\) would have as many cosets as \(G\) has elements (one for each element of \(g\)), but this is not the case.

For \(G\) take the additive group of integers \(\Z\). [...]

In symbols, we can denote the set of all cosets of a subgroup \(H\) as \({\rm Cosets}[H]\), and we can write the set of all cosets of \(H\) as

\[{\rm Cosets}[H] := \{ gH\ |\ g \in G\}\]

which reads "\({\rm Cosets}[H]\) is defined as the set of all sets of the form \(gH\), as \(g\) ranges over \(G\)". We can also build the set of all cosets of all subgroups of \(G\), but let’s not get too carried away.

Sometimes \({\rm Cosets}[H]\) will form a group under the operation it inherits from the parent group \(G\), in which case the group \({\rm Cosets}[H]\) is written \(G / H\), which reads "\(G\) modulo \(H\)". The group \(G / H\) is then called "the quotient group of \(G\) by \(H\)", or the "quotient group of \(G\) modulo \(H\)". Whether \({\rm Cosets}[H]\) is a group or not hinges on a particular property that \(H\) must satisfy.

Three lemmas

So, how do we prove Lagrange’s theorem? We’ll be using a few technical lemmas.

Let \(G\) be a group, let \(H \subseteq G\) be a subgroup. The cosets of \(H\) form a partition of \(G\).

Let \(G\) be a group, let \(H \subseteq G\) be a subgroup, let \(g \in G\). If \(g \notin H\), then \(H\) is disjoint from the coset \(gH\).
